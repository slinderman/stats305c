Dirichlet Process Mixture Models

Prerequisites: This chapter builds on Mixture Models, Markov Chain Monte Carlo (collapsed Gibbs sampling), Gaussian Processes, and Poisson Processes (Poisson random measure construction).

Finite Bayesian mixture models require specifying the number of components KKK in advance. Dirichlet process mixture models (DPMMs) are a Bayesian nonparametric alternative that allows the number of clusters to grow with the data. In this chapter we derive DPMMs from finite mixture models by taking K→∞K \to \inftyK→∞, then study the random measure perspective and its connection to Poisson processes.

Finite Bayesian Mixture Models¶

A finite Bayesian mixture model with KKK components is defined by the following generative process:

  1. Sample mixing proportions from a Dirichlet prior with concentration α∈R+K\boldsymbol{\alpha} \in \mathbb{R}_+^Kα∈R+K​:

π∼Dirichlet(α).\boldsymbol{\pi} \sim \mathrm{Dirichlet}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}).π∼Dirichlet(α).
(1)

  1. Sample component parameters:

θk∼iidp(θ∣ϕ,ν)k=1,…,K.\boldsymbol{\theta}_k \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \boldsymbol{\phi}, \nu) \qquad k = 1, \ldots, K.θk​∼iidp(θ∣ϕ,ν)k=1,…,K.
(2)

  1. Sample assignments and observations:

zn∼iidπ,xn∼p(x∣θzn)n=1,…,N.z_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \boldsymbol{\pi}, \qquad \mathbf{x}_n \sim p(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_{z_n}) \qquad n = 1, \ldots, N.zn​∼iidπ,xn​∼p(x∣θzn​​)n=1,…,N.
(3)

The joint distribution factorizes as:

p(π,{θk},{zn,xn})=Dir(π∣α)∏k=1Kp(θk∣ϕ,ν)∏n=1N∏k=1K[πk p(xn∣θk)]I[zn=k].p(\boldsymbol{\pi}, \{\boldsymbol{\theta}_k\}, \{z_n, \mathbf{x}_n\}) = \mathrm{Dir}(\boldsymbol{\pi} \mid \boldsymbol{\alpha}) \prod_{k=1}^K p(\boldsymbol{\theta}_k \mid \boldsymbol{\phi}, \nu) \prod_{n=1}^N \prod_{k=1}^K \left[\pi_k \, p(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_k)\right]^{\mathbb{I}[z_n = k]}.p(π,{θk​},{zn​,xn​})=Dir(π∣α)k=1∏K​p(θk​∣ϕ,ν)n=1∏N​k=1∏K​[πk​p(xn​∣θk​)]I[zn​=k].
(4)

Collapsing Out Parameters¶

When the likelihood is an exponential family and the prior is its conjugate,

p(x∣θk)=h(x)exp⁡{⟨t(x),θk⟩−A(θk)},p(θk∣ϕ,ν)=1Zθ(ϕ,ν)exp⁡{⟨ϕ,θk⟩−νA(θk)},p(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_k) = h(\mathbf{x}) \exp\{\langle t(\mathbf{x}), \boldsymbol{\theta}_k \rangle - A(\boldsymbol{\theta}_k)\}, \qquad p(\boldsymbol{\theta}_k \mid \boldsymbol{\phi}, \nu) = \frac{1}{Z_\theta(\boldsymbol{\phi}, \nu)} \exp\{\langle \boldsymbol{\phi}, \boldsymbol{\theta}_k \rangle - \nu A(\boldsymbol{\theta}_k)\},p(x∣θk​)=h(x)exp{⟨t(x),θk​⟩−A(θk​)},p(θk​∣ϕ,ν)=Zθ​(ϕ,ν)1​exp{⟨ϕ,θk​⟩−νA(θk​)},
(5)

we can marginalize (or collapse) the component parameters and mixture proportions in closed form.

After integrating out {θk}\{\boldsymbol{\theta}_k\}{θk​} and π\boldsymbol{\pi}π, the marginal likelihood over assignments {zn}\{z_n\}{zn​} and observations {xn}\{\mathbf{x}_n\}{xn​} is:

p({zn,xn}∣ϕ,ν,α)=Zπ(α′)Zπ(α)∏k=1KZθ(ϕk′,νk′)Zθ(ϕ,ν),p(\{z_n, \mathbf{x}_n\} \mid \boldsymbol{\phi}, \nu, \boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \frac{Z_\pi(\boldsymbol{\alpha}')}{Z_\pi(\boldsymbol{\alpha})} \prod_{k=1}^K \frac{Z_\theta(\boldsymbol{\phi}_k', \nu_k')}{Z_\theta(\boldsymbol{\phi}, \nu)},p({zn​,xn​}∣ϕ,ν,α)=Zπ​(α)Zπ​(α′)​k=1∏K​Zθ​(ϕ,ν)Zθ​(ϕk′​,νk′​)​,
(6)

where the updated hyperparameters are:

α′=[α1+N1,…,αK+NK],ϕk′=ϕ+∑n: zn=kt(xn),νk′=ν+Nk,\boldsymbol{\alpha}' = [\alpha_1 + N_1, \ldots, \alpha_K + N_K], \qquad \boldsymbol{\phi}_k' = \boldsymbol{\phi} + \sum_{n:\, z_n=k} t(\mathbf{x}_n), \qquad \nu_k' = \nu + N_k,α′=[α1​+N1​,…,αK​+NK​],ϕk′​=ϕ+n:zn​=k∑​t(xn​),νk′​=ν+Nk​,
(7)

and Zπ(α)=∏kΓ(αk)/Γ(∑kαk)Z_\pi(\boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \prod_k \Gamma(\alpha_k) / \Gamma(\sum_k \alpha_k)Zπ​(α)=∏k​Γ(αk​)/Γ(∑k​αk​) is the Dirichlet normalizing constant.

This is a general pattern: in conjugate exponential families, marginal likelihoods are ratios of posterior to prior normalizing functions.

Collapsed Gibbs Sampling¶

Working with the collapsed distribution enables efficient collapsed Gibbs sampling. The conditional distribution of znz_nzn​, holding all other assignments fixed, simplifies to:

p(zn=k∣xn,{zn′}n′≠n,ϕ,ν,α)∝(αk+Nk(¬n))⏟cluster size⋅p(xn∣{xn′:zn′=k},ϕ,ν)⏟posterior predictive,p(z_n = k \mid \mathbf{x}_n, \{z_{n'}\}_{n' \neq n}, \boldsymbol{\phi}, \nu, \boldsymbol{\alpha}) \propto \underbrace{(\alpha_k + N_k^{(\neg n)})}_{\text{cluster size}} \cdot \underbrace{p(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \{\mathbf{x}_{n'} : z_{n'} = k\}, \boldsymbol{\phi}, \nu)}_{\text{posterior predictive}},p(zn​=k∣xn​,{zn′​}n′=n​,ϕ,ν,α)∝cluster size(αk​+Nk(¬n)​)​​⋅posterior predictivep(xn​∣{xn′​:zn′​=k},ϕ,ν)​​,
(8)

where Nk(¬n)=∑n′≠nI[zn′=k]N_k^{(\neg n)} = \sum_{n' \neq n} \mathbb{I}[z_{n'} = k]Nk(¬n)​=∑n′=n​I[zn′​=k] is the number of other points in cluster kkk.

The two factors have intuitive interpretations:

  • The cluster size term αk+Nk(¬n)\alpha_k + N_k^{(\neg n)}αk​+Nk(¬n)​ comes from the Dirichlet–Multinomial conjugacy: larger clusters attract new points.

  • The posterior predictive term p(xn∣…)p(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \ldots)p(xn​∣…) measures how well xn\mathbf{x}_nxn​ fits with the other points in cluster kkk.

The Dirichlet Process Mixture Model¶

Now set α=αK1K\boldsymbol{\alpha} = \frac{\alpha}{K} \mathbf{1}_Kα=Kα​1K​ and take K→∞K \to \inftyK→∞. In this limit, the collapsed Gibbs update simplifies to:

p(zn=k∣xn,{zn′}n′≠n)∝{Nk(¬n)⋅p(xn∣{xn′:zn′=k},ϕ,ν)if k is an existing clusterα⋅p(xn∣ϕ,ν)if k is a new cluster.p(z_n = k \mid \mathbf{x}_n, \{z_{n'}\}_{n' \neq n}) \propto \begin{cases} N_k^{(\neg n)} \cdot p(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \{\mathbf{x}_{n'} : z_{n'} = k\}, \boldsymbol{\phi}, \nu) & \text{if } k \text{ is an existing cluster} \\ \alpha \cdot p(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\phi}, \nu) & \text{if } k \text{ is a new cluster.} \end{cases}p(zn​=k∣xn​,{zn′​}n′=n​)∝{Nk(¬n)​⋅p(xn​∣{xn′​:zn′​=k},ϕ,ν)α⋅p(xn​∣ϕ,ν)​if k is an existing clusterif k is a new cluster.​
(9)

This is the DPMM collapsed Gibbs update. It is nonparametric — KKK is never fixed, and the number of occupied clusters can grow or shrink at each step:

  • A cluster is created when a data point is assigned to a new cluster (probability controlled by α\alphaα).

  • A cluster is deleted when its last data point is reassigned elsewhere.

The parameter α>0\alpha > 0α>0 is the concentration: larger α\alphaα creates more clusters.

The Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP)¶

The prior on partitions induced by the DPMM has a beautiful sequential description called the Chinese restaurant process (CRP). Imagine NNN customers entering a restaurant one at a time:

  • Customer 1 sits at a new table.

  • Customer nnn sits at an existing table kkk with probability ∣Ck∣α+n−1\frac{|\mathcal{C}_k|}{\alpha + n - 1}α+n−1∣Ck​∣​, or starts a new table with probability αα+n−1\frac{\alpha}{\alpha + n - 1}α+n−1α​.

The resulting partition C={C1,…}\mathcal{C} = \{\mathcal{C}_1, \ldots\}C={C1​,…} is the CRP prior on partitions of [N][N][N].

Stick-Breaking Construction¶

The DPMM can also be defined directly via a random measure:

Θ=∑k=1∞πk δθk,θk∼iidG,\Theta = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \pi_k \, \delta_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_k}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\theta}_k \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} G,Θ=k=1∑∞​πk​δθk​​,θk​∼iidG,
(10)

where the weights follow a stick-breaking construction: sample ℓk∼Beta(1,α)\ell_k \sim \mathrm{Beta}(1, \alpha)ℓk​∼Beta(1,α) and set

πk=ℓk∏j=1k−1(1−ℓj).\pi_k = \ell_k \prod_{j=1}^{k-1} (1 - \ell_j).πk​=ℓk​j=1∏k−1​(1−ℓj​).
(11)

We write Θ∼DP(α,G)\Theta \sim \mathrm{DP}(\alpha, G)Θ∼DP(α,G) and call α\alphaα the concentration and GGG the base measure.

def normal_log_posterior_predictive(x, cluster_points, mu0, kappa0, alpha0, beta0):
    '''Log posterior predictive p(x | cluster_points) under Normal-NormalInverseGamma prior.

    Model: x_n | mu, sigma^2 ~ N(mu, sigma^2)
           mu | sigma^2 ~ N(mu0, sigma^2 / kappa0)
           sigma^2 ~ IG(alpha0, beta0)

    The marginal p(x | data) is a Student-t distribution.

    Args:
        x: new point, shape (D,)
        cluster_points: tensor of shape (N_k, D); empty if prior predictive
        mu0: prior mean, shape (D,)
        kappa0: prior precision scaling (scalar)
        alpha0: prior shape (scalar)
        beta0: prior rate (scalar)
    Returns:
        log predictive density (scalar)
    '''
    D = x.shape[0]
    n = len(cluster_points)
    if n > 0:
        x_bar = cluster_points.mean(0)
        kappa_n = kappa0 + n
        mu_n = (kappa0 * mu0 + n * x_bar) / kappa_n
        alpha_n = alpha0 + n / 2.0
        S = ((cluster_points - x_bar) ** 2).sum(0)  # shape (D,)
        diff = x_bar - mu0
        beta_n = beta0 + 0.5 * S + 0.5 * kappa0 * n / kappa_n * diff ** 2
    else:
        kappa_n, mu_n, alpha_n, beta_n = kappa0, mu0, alpha0, beta0 * torch.ones(D)

    # Marginal is a Student-t: t(2*alpha_n, mu_n, beta_n*(kappa_n+1)/(alpha_n*kappa_n))
    df = 2.0 * alpha_n
    scale2 = beta_n * (kappa_n + 1) / (alpha_n * kappa_n)
    log_p = 0.0
    for d in range(D):
        log_p += float(student_t.logpdf(x[d].item(), df=df, loc=mu_n[d].item(),
                                         scale=scale2[d].item() ** 0.5))
    return log_p


def collapsed_gibbs_dpmm(X, alpha, mu0, kappa0, alpha0, beta0, num_iters=100, seed=0):
    '''Collapsed Gibbs sampler for a DPMM with Normal-NormalInverseGamma components.

    Args:
        X: data, shape (N, D)
        alpha: DP concentration
        mu0, kappa0, alpha0, beta0: NIG prior hyperparameters
        num_iters: number of Gibbs sweeps
        seed: random seed
    Returns:
        z: final cluster assignments, shape (N,)
        K_history: number of clusters at each iteration
    '''
    torch.manual_seed(seed)
    N, D = X.shape
    mu0 = mu0 * torch.ones(D)

    # Initialize: one cluster per data point
    z = torch.arange(N)
    K_history = []

    for it in range(num_iters):
        for n in range(N):
            # Remove n from its current cluster
            z_n = z[n].item()
            z[n] = -1
            mask_n = (z >= 0)
            unique_ks = z[mask_n].unique().tolist()

            # Compute log probabilities for each existing cluster and a new one
            log_probs = []
            labels = []
            for k in unique_ks:
                pts = X[(z == k)]
                N_k = len(pts)
                log_like = normal_log_posterior_predictive(X[n], pts, mu0, kappa0, alpha0, beta0)
                log_probs.append(np.log(N_k) + log_like)
                labels.append(k)

            # New cluster
            log_like_new = normal_log_posterior_predictive(X[n], X[:0], mu0, kappa0, alpha0, beta0)
            log_probs.append(np.log(alpha) + log_like_new)
            labels.append(max(unique_ks + [-1]) + 1 if unique_ks else 0)

            # Sample
            log_probs = np.array(log_probs)
            log_probs -= log_probs.max()
            probs = np.exp(log_probs)
            probs /= probs.sum()
            chosen = np.random.choice(len(labels), p=probs)
            z[n] = labels[chosen]

        # Re-label clusters 0, 1, 2, ...
        unique_labels = z.unique().tolist()
        remap = {old: new for new, old in enumerate(unique_labels)}
        z = torch.tensor([remap[zi.item()] for zi in z])
        K_history.append(len(unique_labels))

    return z, K_history

Extensions and Related Models¶

Pitman–Yor Process¶

The Pitman–Yor process Orbanz, 2014 generalizes the DP by adding a discount parameter d∈[0,1)d \in [0, 1)d∈[0,1). We write Θ∼PYP(α,d,G)\Theta \sim \mathrm{PYP}(\alpha, d, G)Θ∼PYP(α,d,G) where the weights use the modified stick-breaking:

ℓk∼Beta(1−d, α+kd),πk=ℓk∏j=1k−1(1−ℓj).\ell_k \sim \mathrm{Beta}(1 - d, \, \alpha + kd), \qquad \pi_k = \ell_k \prod_{j=1}^{k-1}(1 - \ell_j).ℓk​∼Beta(1−d,α+kd),πk​=ℓk​j=1∏k−1​(1−ℓj​).
(12)

When d=0d = 0d=0 we recover the DP. When d>0d > 0d>0, the cluster sizes follow a power law, making the PYP well-suited to linguistic data where word frequencies exhibit Zipfian behavior.

The CRP analogue assigns customer nnn to existing table kkk with probability ∣Ck∣−dα+n−1\frac{|\mathcal{C}_k| - d}{\alpha + n - 1}α+n−1∣Ck​∣−d​ or to a new table with probability α+d⋅K(n)α+n−1\frac{\alpha + d \cdot K^{(n)}}{\alpha + n - 1}α+n−1α+d⋅K(n)​, where K(n)K^{(n)}K(n) is the current number of tables.

Mixture of Finite Mixtures¶

DPMMs are often used to select KKK automatically, but the DP almost surely generates infinitely many clusters as N→∞N \to \inftyN→∞. When the true number of clusters is finite but unknown, mixture of finite mixture models (MFMMs) Miller & Harrison, 2018 are more appropriate:

K∼p(K),π∼Dir(α1K),θk∼iidG,zn∼iidπ,xn∼p(x∣θzn).K \sim p(K), \quad \boldsymbol{\pi} \sim \mathrm{Dir}(\alpha \mathbf{1}_K), \quad \boldsymbol{\theta}_k \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} G, \quad z_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \boldsymbol{\pi}, \quad \mathbf{x}_n \sim p(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_{z_n}).K∼p(K),π∼Dir(α1K​),θk​∼iidG,zn​∼iidπ,xn​∼p(x∣θzn​​).
(13)

A natural choice is K−1∼Poisson(λ)K - 1 \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda)K−1∼Poisson(λ). Collapsed Gibbs samplers for MFMMs have a similar form to the DPMM sampler but converge to a finite KKK.

Poisson Random Measures¶

Dirichlet processes and Poisson processes are deeply connected. In fact, DPs are instances of completely random measures (CRMs) constructed from Poisson processes.

Random Measures¶

A random measure on RD\mathbb{R}^DRD is a measure-valued random variable. An atomic random measure takes the form:

μ=∑k=1∞wk δθk,\mu = \sum_{k=1}^\infty w_k \, \delta_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_k},μ=k=1∑∞​wk​δθk​​,
(14)

where the weights wk∈R+w_k \in \mathbb{R}_+wk​∈R+​ and locations θk∈RD\boldsymbol{\theta}_k \in \mathbb{R}^Dθk​∈RD are random.

Poisson Random Measures¶

Construct a random measure by sampling weight–location pairs from a marked Poisson process on R+×RD\mathbb{R}_+ \times \mathbb{R}^DR+​×RD:

{(wk,θk)}k=1∞∼PP(λ(w,θ)).\{(w_k, \boldsymbol{\theta}_k)\}_{k=1}^\infty \sim \mathrm{PP}(\lambda(w, \boldsymbol{\theta})).{(wk​,θk​)}k=1∞​∼PP(λ(w,θ)).
(15)

For a homogeneous measure the intensity factors: λ(w,θ)=λ(w)⋅g(θ)\lambda(w, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \lambda(w) \cdot g(\boldsymbol{\theta})λ(w,θ)=λ(w)⋅g(θ), where ggg is a density on RD\mathbb{R}^DRD (the base measure GGG).

The Gamma Process and Dirichlet Process¶

Choose the weight intensity

λ(w)=αw−1e−βw.\lambda(w) = \alpha w^{-1} e^{-\beta w}.λ(w)=αw−1e−βw.
(16)

Then ∫0∞λ(w) dw=∞\int_0^\infty \lambda(w)\, dw = \infty∫0∞​λ(w)dw=∞, so μ\muμ has infinitely many atoms. Nevertheless, the total mass W=∑kwk∼Gamma(α,1/β)W = \sum_k w_k \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha, 1/\beta)W=∑k​wk​∼Gamma(α,1/β) is almost surely finite. This is the gamma process μ∼GaP(α,G)\mu \sim \mathrm{GaP}(\alpha, G)μ∼GaP(α,G).

Normalizing gives the Dirichlet process:

μ∼GaP(α,G)⟹Θ=μW=∑k=1∞wkW δθk∼DP(α,G).\mu \sim \mathrm{GaP}(\alpha, G) \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Theta = \frac{\mu}{W} = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{w_k}{W} \, \delta_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_k} \sim \mathrm{DP}(\alpha, G).μ∼GaP(α,G)⟹Θ=Wμ​=k=1∑∞​Wwk​​δθk​​∼DP(α,G).
(17)

This is a key result: Dirichlet processes are normalized gamma processes.

Other Completely Random Measures¶

Different weight intensities yield other useful random measures:

Weight intensity λ(w)\lambda(w)λ(w)Name
αw−1e−βw\alpha w^{-1} e^{-\beta w}αw−1e−βwGamma process → DP (after normalization)
γw−(α+1)\gamma w^{-(\alpha+1)}γw−(α+1)Stable process
γw−1(1−w)α−1\gamma w^{-1}(1-w)^{\alpha-1}γw−1(1−w)α−1Beta process

A CRM μ\muμ has the remarkable property that Θ=μ/W\Theta = \mu/WΘ=μ/W is independent of WWW if and only if μ\muμ is a gamma process — the DP is the unique normalized CRM with this independence property Orbanz, 2014.
