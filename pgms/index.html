Probabilistic Graphical Models

Prerequisites

This chapter builds on Bayesian Modeling. It develops the graphical model language used throughout the course.

Directed graphical models (DGMs) provide a language for representing joint distributions over many variables compactly. By encoding conditional independence assumptions as a directed acyclic graph (DAG), we can specify and reason about high-dimensional distributions that would otherwise be intractable.

In this lecture we cover:

  • Product-rule factorization and the DAG representation

  • Plate notation for repeated structure

  • Conditional independence and the Markov blanket

  • Exchangeability and de Finetti’s theorem

Directed Graphical Models¶

Motivation¶

Consider a joint distribution p(x1,…,xD)p(x_1, \ldots, x_D)p(x1​,…,xD​) over DDD discrete variables, each taking values in {1,…,K}\{1, \ldots, K\}{1,…,K}. An arbitrary distribution on DDD such variables requires KD−1K^D - 1KD−1 parameters — exponential in DDD. Even for modest DDD and KKK this is enormous.

The product rule always lets us factor any joint distribution as a chain,

p(x)=p(x1) p(x2∣x1) p(x3∣x1,x2)⋯p(xD∣x1,…,xD−1).\begin{align} p(\mbx) &= p(x_1) \, p(x_2 \mid x_1) \, p(x_3 \mid x_1, x_2) \cdots p(x_D \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{D-1}). \end{align}p(x)​=p(x1​)p(x2​∣x1​)p(x3​∣x1​,x2​)⋯p(xD​∣x1​,…,xD−1​).​​
(1)

But this representation is equally expensive: conditioning on all preceding variables.

The key idea of graphical models is that many conditional independencies in real-world distributions make most of these conditions unnecessary. If xdx_dxd​ only depends on a small subset pad⊆{1,…,d−1}\mathsf{pa}_d \subseteq \{1, \ldots, d-1\}pad​⊆{1,…,d−1} of the preceding variables (its parents), then

p(x)=∏d=1Dp(xd∣xpad).\begin{align} p(\mbx) &= \prod_{d=1}^D p(x_d \mid \mbx_{\mathsf{pa}_d}). \end{align}p(x)​=d=1∏D​p(xd​∣xpad​​).​​
(2)

The number of parameters is now ∑d=1DK∣pad∣\sum_{d=1}^D K^{|\mathsf{pa}_d|}∑d=1D​K∣pad​∣, which is manageable when ∣pad∣|\mathsf{pa}_d|∣pad​∣ is small.

Definition¶

A directed graphical model (DGM), or Bayesian network, represents this factorization as a directed acyclic graph (DAG):

  • Each node corresponds to a variable xdx_dxd​ — it may be discrete or continuous, scalar or multidimensional.

  • A directed edge from node iii to node jjj means i∈paji \in \mathsf{pa}_ji∈paj​.

  • The graph must be acyclic (no directed cycles), which ensures the product factorization is well-defined.

The absence of an edge from iii to jjj encodes a conditional independence assumption: xjx_jxj​ does not depend directly on xix_ixi​ (given xjx_jxj​’s other parents). The more edges we omit, the more compact the representation.

Plate Notation¶

Many probabilistic models have repeated structure — the same conditional distribution applied to many variables. For example, the diagonal-covariance Gaussian,

N(x∣μ,σ2I)=∏d=1DN(xd∣μd,σ2),\begin{align} \cN(\mbx \mid \mbmu, \sigma^2 \mbI) &= \prod_{d=1}^D \cN(x_d \mid \mu_d, \sigma^2), \end{align}N(x∣μ,σ2I)​=d=1∏D​N(xd​∣μd​,σ2),​​
(4)

describes DDD independent variables sharing the same conditional form. Drawing DDD separate nodes would be redundant.

Plate notation compresses repeated structure by drawing a single representative node inside a rectangle (the plate), labelled with the index set. A node outside the plate is shared across all repetitions; a node inside is replicated once per index.

Plates can be nested: a plate labelled n=1,…,Nn = 1, \ldots, Nn=1,…,N inside a plate labelled s=1,…,Ss = 1, \ldots, Ss=1,…,S represents N×SN \times SN×S variables, one for each (n,s)(n, s)(n,s) pair. We will use nested plates extensively in the hierarchical model below.

Conditional Independence¶

We write xi⊥ ⁣ ⁣ ⁣⊥xj∣xsx_i \perp\!\!\!\perp x_j \mid \mbx_sxi​⊥⊥xj​∣xs​ if xix_ixi​ and xjx_jxj​ are conditionally independent given xs\mbx_sxs​:

p(xi∣xj,xs)=p(xi∣xs).\begin{align} p(x_i \mid x_j, \mbx_s) &= p(x_i \mid \mbx_s). \end{align}p(xi​∣xj​,xs​)​=p(xi​∣xs​).​​
(5)

Conditional independencies in a DGM can be read off by examining three basic motifs (see Murphy or Bishop for the full d-separation rules):

  1. Chain xi→xk→xjx_i \to x_k \to x_jxi​→xk​→xj​: xi⊥ ⁣ ⁣ ⁣⊥xj∣xkx_i \perp\!\!\!\perp x_j \mid x_kxi​⊥⊥xj​∣xk​ (conditioning on the middle node blocks the path).

  2. Fork xi←xk→xjx_i \leftarrow x_k \to x_jxi​←xk​→xj​: xi⊥ ⁣ ⁣ ⁣⊥xj∣xkx_i \perp\!\!\!\perp x_j \mid x_kxi​⊥⊥xj​∣xk​ (conditioning on the common cause blocks the path).

  3. Collider xi→xk←xjx_i \to x_k \leftarrow x_jxi​→xk​←xj​: xi⊥̸ ⁣ ⁣ ⁣⊥xj∣xkx_i \not\perp\!\!\!\perp x_j \mid x_kxi​⊥⊥xj​∣xk​ (conditioning on a common effect opens a previously blocked path — often called explaining away).

Markov Blanket¶

The Markov blanket of a node xdx_dxd​ is the set of variables that, when conditioned on, render xdx_dxd​ independent of all other variables. In a DGM, the Markov blanket of xdx_dxd​ consists of:

  • xdx_dxd​’s parents,

  • xdx_dxd​’s children, and

  • the other parents of xdx_dxd​’s children (the co-parents).

These are exactly the variables that appear alongside xdx_dxd​ in at least one factor of the joint distribution. Given its Markov blanket, xdx_dxd​ is conditionally independent of all remaining variables — a fact we will exploit repeatedly in deriving conditional posteriors.

Exchangeability and de Finetti’s Theorem¶

Suppose we want to model a collection of variables (x1,…,xN)(x_1, \ldots, x_N)(x1​,…,xN​) and have no information that distinguishes or orders them. A natural requirement is exchangeability: the joint distribution is invariant to any permutation π\piπ,

p(x1,…,xN)=p(xπ(1),…,xπ(N)).\begin{align} p(x_1, \ldots, x_N) &= p(x_{\pi(1)}, \ldots, x_{\pi(N)}). \end{align}p(x1​,…,xN​)​=p(xπ(1)​,…,xπ(N)​).​​
(6)

The simplest exchangeable distribution assumes the variables are i.i.d., p(x)=∏np(xn)p(\mbx) = \prod_n p(x_n)p(x)=∏n​p(xn​). More generally, we may assume the variables are conditionally independent given a latent parameter θ\thetaθ which is marginalized over,

p(x1,…,xN)=∫[∏n=1Np(xn∣θ)]p(θ) ⁣dθ.\begin{align} p(x_1, \ldots, x_N) &= \int \left[\prod_{n=1}^N p(x_n \mid \theta)\right] p(\theta) \dif \theta. \end{align}p(x1​,…,xN​)​=∫[n=1∏N​p(xn​∣θ)]p(θ)dθ.​​
(7)

Marginally, the xnx_nxn​ are not independent — observing some xnx_nxn​ updates our belief about θ\thetaθ and therefore about other xn′x_{n'}xn′​. But they are exchangeable.

De Finetti’s theorem states that as N→∞N \to \inftyN→∞, any suitably well-behaved exchangeable distribution on (x1,…,xN)(x_1, \ldots, x_N)(x1​,…,xN​) can be represented in exactly this mixture form. The theorem does not hold in general for finite NNN, but it provides a compelling motivation for Bayesian hierarchical models: placing a prior over θ\thetaθ and conditioning on the data is the correct procedure when observations are exchangeable.

Conclusion¶

Directed graphical models provide a principled way to represent the conditional independence structure of a joint distribution. The key results are:

  • Any joint distribution factors as a product of conditionals over a DAG; absent edges encode conditional independence assumptions that reduce the number of parameters.

  • Plate notation compresses repeated structure and makes hierarchical models easy to read.

  • The Markov blanket of a node determines its complete conditional — the ingredient needed for Gibbs sampling (Lecture 5).

  • Exchangeability motivates placing a shared prior over group-level parameters, and de Finetti’s theorem gives this practice a theoretical foundation.
