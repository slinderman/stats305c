Variational Autoencoders

Prerequisites

This chapter builds on Linear Gaussian Latent Variable Models. The reparameterization trick and gradient-based variational inference used here are formalized in the Gradient-Based Variational Inference chapter (Part III). Readers unfamiliar with neural networks and automatic differentiation should consult supplementary resources.

The previous two chapters developed the machinery for gradient-based VI: the ELBO, the reparameterisation trick, and Adam. A Variational Autoencoder (VAE) Kingma & Welling, 2014Rezende et al., 2014 puts these tools to work in a deep generative model. The core idea is simple:

Fit a nonlinear latent variable model by jointly learning a decoder (generative model) and an encoder (approximate posterior) using the reparameterised ELBO.

The result is a model that can both generate new data (by sampling from the prior and decoding) and embed data into a structured latent space (by encoding).

Topics covered:

  • The VAE generative model: from PPCA to deep latent variable models

  • The ELBO as reconstruction loss + KL regulariser

  • Amortised inference: why we use an encoder network instead of per-datapoint variational parameters

  • The complete training algorithm in a single clean loop

  • Code: a VAE on 2D data with a 1D latent space

The Generative Model¶

From PPCA to VAEs¶

Recall Probabilistic PCA from Chapter 6:

zn∼N(0,IH),xn∣zn∼N(Wzn+μ,σ2ID).\mbz_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_H), \qquad \mbx_n \mid \mbz_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{W}\mbz_n + \mbmu, \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}_D).zn​∼N(0,IH​),xn​∣zn​∼N(Wzn​+μ,σ2ID​).
(1)

The decoder z↦Wz+μ\mbz \mapsto \mathbf{W}\mbz + \mbmuz↦Wz+μ is linear. A VAE replaces it with a neural network:

zn∼N(0,IH),xn∣zn∼p(x∣g(zn; θ)),\mbz_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_H), \qquad \mbx_n \mid \mbz_n \sim p(\mbx \mid g(\mbz_n;\, \mbtheta)),zn​∼N(0,IH​),xn​∣zn​∼p(x∣g(zn​;θ)),
(2)

where g:RH→RDg : \mathbb{R}^H \to \mathbb{R}^Dg:RH→RD is a multilayer perceptron (MLP) with parameters θ\mbthetaθ, and p(x∣g(zn;θ))p(\mbx \mid g(\mbz_n; \mbtheta))p(x∣g(zn​;θ)) is a suitable observation model (Gaussian for real data, Bernoulli for binary data, etc.).

The prior p(zn)=N(0,I)p(\mbz_n) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})p(zn​)=N(0,I) acts as a regulariser: it encourages the latent space to be compact and well-organised.

Why Is the Posterior Intractable?¶

In PPCA the posterior p(zn∣xn)p(\mbz_n \mid \mbx_n)p(zn​∣xn​) is Gaussian and has a closed form. In a VAE, the nonlinear decoder means

p(zn∣xn;θ)∝p(xn∣g(zn;θ)) p(zn)p(\mbz_n \mid \mbx_n; \mbtheta) \propto p(\mbx_n \mid g(\mbz_n; \mbtheta))\, p(\mbz_n)p(zn​∣xn​;θ)∝p(xn​∣g(zn​;θ))p(zn​)
(3)

is no longer a standard distribution — the normalising constant requires integrating over zn\mbz_nzn​, which is intractable. This is where variational inference comes in.

The ELBO: Reconstruction + KL¶

Introduce a variational approximation q(zn;λn)q(\mbz_n; \mblambda_n)q(zn​;λn​) to the intractable posterior. The ELBO for data point nnn is:

Ln(λn,θ)=Eq(zn; λn) ⁣[log⁡p(xn∣zn;θ)]−DKL(q(zn;λn) ∥ p(zn)).\mathcal{L}_n(\mblambda_n, \mbtheta) = \mathbb{E}_{q(\mbz_n;\,\mblambda_n)}\!\bigl[ \log p(\mbx_n \mid \mbz_n; \mbtheta) \bigr] - D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q(\mbz_n; \mblambda_n) \,\|\, p(\mbz_n)\bigr).Ln​(λn​,θ)=Eq(zn​;λn​)​[logp(xn​∣zn​;θ)]−DKL​(q(zn​;λn​)∥p(zn​)).
(4)

This decomposition has a clean interpretation:

TermNameRole
Eq[log⁡p(xn∣zn;θ)]\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(\mbx_n \mid \mbz_n; \mbtheta)]Eq​[logp(xn​∣zn​;θ)]ReconstructionHow well can we recover xn\mbx_nxn​ from its encoding zn\mbz_nzn​?
DKL(q∣p)D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q | p)DKL​(q∣p)KL regulariserHow far is the approximate posterior from the prior?

Maximising the ELBO encourages good reconstructions while keeping the latent codes close to the prior — the same tension as a regularised autoencoder.

Gaussian Encoder and Decoder¶

Use a diagonal Gaussian encoder q(zn;λn)=N(μn,diag(σn2))q(\mbz_n; \mblambda_n) = \mathcal{N}(\mbmu_n, \mathrm{diag}(\mbsigma_n^2))q(zn​;λn​)=N(μn​,diag(σn2​)). The KL term then has a closed form:

DKL(N(μn,diag(σn2)) ∥ N(0,I))=12∑h=1H(μn,h2+σn,h2−1−log⁡σn,h2).D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(\mathcal{N}(\mbmu_n, \mathrm{diag}(\mbsigma_n^2)) \,\|\, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})\bigr) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{h=1}^H \bigl(\mu_{n,h}^2 + \sigma_{n,h}^2 - 1 - \log \sigma_{n,h}^2\bigr).DKL​(N(μn​,diag(σn2​))∥N(0,I))=21​h=1∑H​(μn,h2​+σn,h2​−1−logσn,h2​).
(5)

For a Gaussian decoder p(xn∣zn)=N(g(zn;θ),I)p(\mbx_n \mid \mbz_n) = \mathcal{N}(g(\mbz_n; \mbtheta), \mathbf{I})p(xn​∣zn​)=N(g(zn​;θ),I), the reconstruction term is just

Eq[log⁡p(xn∣zn;θ)]≈−12∥xn−g(zn(m);θ)∥2+const,zn(m)=μn+σn⊙ϵn,\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(\mbx_n \mid \mbz_n; \mbtheta)] \approx -\tfrac{1}{2}\|\mbx_n - g(\mbz_n^{(m)}; \mbtheta)\|^2 + \text{const}, \quad \mbz_n^{(m)} = \mbmu_n + \mbsigma_n \odot \mbepsilon_n,Eq​[logp(xn​∣zn​;θ)]≈−21​∥xn​−g(zn(m)​;θ)∥2+const,zn(m)​=μn​+σn​⊙ϵn​,
(6)

where ϵn∼N(0,I)\mbepsilon_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})ϵn​∼N(0,I) is the reparameterised noise.

Amortised Inference: The Encoder Network¶

The Per-Datapoint Bottleneck¶

With NNN data points we have NNN sets of variational parameters {λn}\{\mblambda_n\}{λn​}. If we optimise each independently — running many gradient steps on λn\mblambda_nλn​ for every update of θ\mbthetaθ — the E-step dominates the computation and the method does not scale.

The Key Observation¶

The optimal variational parameters are a function of the data point:

λn⋆=arg⁡min⁡λn  DKL(q(zn;λn)∥p(zn∣xn;θ))=f⋆(xn,θ).\mblambda_n^\star = \underset{\mblambda_n}{\arg\min}\; D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(\mbz_n; \mblambda_n) \| p(\mbz_n \mid \mbx_n; \mbtheta)) = f^\star(\mbx_n, \mbtheta).λn⋆​=λn​argmin​DKL​(q(zn​;λn​)∥p(zn​∣xn​;θ))=f⋆(xn​,θ).
(7)

Instead of learning NNN independent λn\mblambda_nλn​ vectors, we learn a single encoder network (also called a recognition network or inference network) f(x;ϕ)f(\mbx; \mbphi)f(x;ϕ) that maps any data point to its variational parameters:

(μn,log⁡σn2)≈f(xn;ϕ).(\mbmu_n, \log \mbsigma_n^2) \approx f(\mbx_n; \mbphi).(μn​,logσn2​)≈f(xn​;ϕ).
(8)

Amortisation means we amortise the cost of inference across the entire dataset: one forward pass through the encoder gives λn\mblambda_nλn​ instantly, at the cost of a slightly suboptimal approximation.

The Amortisation Gap¶

The encoder can only represent functions in the parametric class defined by its architecture. This introduces an amortisation gap:

amortisation gap=min⁡λnDKL(q(zn;λn)∥p(zn∣xn))  −  DKL(q(zn;f(xn;ϕ))∥p(zn∣xn)).\text{amortisation gap} = \min_{\mblambda_n} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(\mbz_n; \mblambda_n) \| p(\mbz_n \mid \mbx_n)) \;-\; D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(\mbz_n; f(\mbx_n; \mbphi)) \| p(\mbz_n \mid \mbx_n)).amortisation gap=λn​min​DKL​(q(zn​;λn​)∥p(zn​∣xn​))−DKL​(q(zn​;f(xn​;ϕ))∥p(zn​∣xn​)).
(9)

There is also an approximation gap from restricting qqq to the diagonal-Gaussian family. Cremer et al., 2018 study both gaps empirically.

The VAE Training Algorithm¶

With an encoder, the ELBO for data point nnn becomes a function of both θ\mbthetaθ (decoder) and ϕ\mbphiϕ (encoder):

Ln(θ,ϕ)=Eϵ∼N(0,I) ⁣[log⁡p(xn∣g(μϕ(xn)+σϕ(xn)⊙ϵ⏟zn;θ))]−DKL(N(μϕ(xn),diag(σϕ2(xn))) ∥ N(0,I)).\mathcal{L}_n(\mbtheta, \mbphi) = \mathbb{E}_{\mbepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I})}\!\Bigl[ \log p\bigl(\mbx_n \mid g(\underbrace{\mbmu_\phi(\mbx_n) + \mbsigma_\phi(\mbx_n) \odot \mbepsilon}_{\mbz_n}; \mbtheta)\bigr) \Bigr] - D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(\mathcal{N}(\mbmu_\phi(\mbx_n), \mathrm{diag}(\mbsigma_\phi^2(\mbx_n))) \,\|\, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})\bigr).Ln​(θ,ϕ)=Eϵ∼N(0,I)​[logp(xn​∣g(zn​μϕ​(xn​)+σϕ​(xn​)⊙ϵ​​;θ))]−DKL​(N(μϕ​(xn​),diag(σϕ2​(xn​)))∥N(0,I)).
(10)

Because zn\mbz_nzn​ is reparameterised through ϕ\mbphiϕ via μϕ\mbmu_\phiμϕ​ and σϕ\mbsigma_\phiσϕ​, the gradient ∇ϕLn\nabla_{\mbphi} \mathcal{L}_n∇ϕ​Ln​ flows through the encoder by standard backpropagation.

The complete algorithm — no separate E and M steps:

repeat:
    sample mini-batch {x_n} ⊂ {x_1, …, x_N}
    for each x_n in the mini-batch:
        compute (μ_φ(x_n), σ_φ(x_n)) via encoder forward pass
        sample ε ~ N(0, I);  z_n = μ_φ(x_n) + σ_φ(x_n) ⊙ ε
        compute ELBO_n = log p(x_n | g(z_n; θ)) − KL term
    loss = −mean(ELBO_n)
    loss.backward()             # gradients wrt both θ and φ
    optimiser.step()            # update θ and φ together

The encoder and decoder are trained jointly in a single optimisation loop — there is no explicit E-step / M-step separation.

Linear VAE = PPCA

When both encoder and decoder are linear (g(z)=Wz+μg(\mbz) = \mathbf{W}\mbz + \mbmug(z)=Wz+μ, f(x)=(Ax+b,c)f(\mbx) = (\mathbf{A}\mbx + \mbb, \mathbf{c})f(x)=(Ax+b,c)), the optimal encoder is exactly the PPCA posterior p(z∣x)=N(J−1h,J−1)p(\mbz \mid \mbx) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{J}^{-1}\mbh, \mathbf{J}^{-1})p(z∣x)=N(J−1h,J−1) from Chapter 6. The VAE is PPCA with a neural-network upgrade.

# ── Dataset: 2D observations on a noisy 1D manifold (sine curve) ─────────────
torch.manual_seed(305)
N = 1000
t_true = torch.linspace(-3, 3, N) + 0.05 * torch.randn(N)  # latent "phase"
X = torch.stack([t_true + 0.15 * torch.randn(N),
                 torch.sin(t_true) + 0.15 * torch.randn(N)], dim=1)   # (N, 2)
# Colour points by their true phase for later visualisation
c_true = t_true.numpy()


# ── Model ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
class Encoder(nn.Module):
    """Maps x ∈ R^D to (μ_z, log σ_z) ∈ R^H × R^H."""
    def __init__(self, D, H, hidden=32):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(D, hidden), nn.Tanh(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden), nn.Tanh(),
        )
        self.mu_head    = nn.Linear(hidden, H)
        self.logσ_head  = nn.Linear(hidden, H)

    def forward(self, x):
        h = self.net(x)
        return self.mu_head(h), self.logσ_head(h)


class Decoder(nn.Module):
    """Maps z ∈ R^H to μ_x ∈ R^D (Gaussian likelihood with unit variance)."""
    def __init__(self, H, D, hidden=32):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(H, hidden), nn.Tanh(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden), nn.Tanh(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, D),
        )

    def forward(self, z):
        return self.net(z)


def elbo(x, encoder, decoder):
    """Single-sample reparameterised ELBO (per data point, mean over batch)."""
    μ_z, logσ_z = encoder(x)           # (B, H)
    σ_z  = logσ_z.exp()

    # Reparameterised sample
    ε    = torch.randn_like(μ_z)
    z    = μ_z + σ_z * ε               # (B, H)

    # Reconstruction: -0.5 ||x - g(z)||^2  (ignoring log 2π constant)
    x_hat    = decoder(z)               # (B, D)
    recon    = -0.5 * ((x - x_hat) ** 2).sum(dim=1).mean()

    # KL[ N(μ_z, diag(σ_z²)) || N(0, I) ]  closed form
    kl = 0.5 * (μ_z**2 + σ_z**2 - 1 - logσ_z * 2).sum(dim=1).mean()

    return recon - kl, recon.item(), kl.item()


# ── Training ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
H, D        = 1, 2          # 1-D latent space, 2-D observations
batch_size  = 128
num_epochs  = 300

encoder = Encoder(D, H, hidden=32)
decoder = Decoder(H, D, hidden=32)
opt     = torch.optim.Adam(list(encoder.parameters()) +
                           list(decoder.parameters()), lr=1e-3)

dataset = torch.utils.data.TensorDataset(X)
loader  = torch.utils.data.DataLoader(dataset, batch_size=batch_size, shuffle=True)

history = {'elbo': [], 'recon': [], 'kl': []}

for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for (xb,) in loader:
        lb, r, k = elbo(xb, encoder, decoder)
        (-lb).backward()
        opt.step(); opt.zero_grad()
    # Track once per epoch on full data
    with torch.no_grad():
        lb_full, r_full, k_full = elbo(X, encoder, decoder)
    history['elbo'].append(lb_full)
    history['recon'].append(r_full)
    history['kl'].append(k_full)

print(f'Final ELBO: {history["elbo"][-1]:.2f}  '
      f'(recon={history["recon"][-1]:.2f}, KL={history["kl"][-1]:.2f})')
Final ELBO: -1.11  (recon=-0.50, KL=0.61)

Conclusion¶

A VAE is a nonlinear latent variable model trained by amortised variational inference.

ComponentRoleParameters
Decoder g(z;θ)g(\mbz; \mbtheta)g(z;θ)Generative model: maps latent → observedθ\mbthetaθ
Encoder f(x;ϕ)f(\mbx; \mbphi)f(x;ϕ)Recognition model: maps observed → (μz,log⁡σz)(\mbmu_z, \log\mbsigma_z)(μz​,logσz​)ϕ\mbphiϕ
Prior p(z)=N(0,I)p(\mbz) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})p(z)=N(0,I)Regulariser on the latent space—

The ELBO has two interpretable terms:

L(θ,ϕ)=Eq[log⁡p(x∣z;θ)]⏟reconstruction−DKL(q(z∣x;ϕ)∥p(z))⏟KL regulariser.\mathcal{L}(\mbtheta, \mbphi) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(\mbx \mid \mbz; \mbtheta)]}_{\text{reconstruction}} - \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(\mbz \mid \mbx; \mbphi) \| p(\mbz))}_{\text{KL regulariser}}.L(θ,ϕ)=reconstructionEq​[logp(x∣z;θ)]​​−KL regulariserDKL​(q(z∣x;ϕ)∥p(z))​​.
(11)

Key takeaways:

  • Amortisation replaces NNN separate optimisation problems (one per data point) with a single shared encoder, enabling scalability.

  • Reparameterisation makes the ELBO differentiable through both the decoder (via θ\mbthetaθ) and the encoder (via ϕ\mbphiϕ), so both can be trained in a single gradient loop.

  • The KL term with a standard normal prior has a closed form for diagonal Gaussian encoders, eliminating MC noise from that term.

  • A linear VAE (linear encoder and decoder) recovers PPCA exactly, so VAEs are a strict generalisation of the linear LVMs from Chapter 6.

  • Two sources of sub-optimality: the approximation gap (diagonal Gaussian may not match the true posterior shape) and the amortisation gap (the encoder may not perfectly represent the optimal λn\mblambda_nλn​ for every xn\mbx_nxn​).
