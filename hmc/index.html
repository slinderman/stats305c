Hamiltonian Monte Carlo

Prerequisites

This chapter builds on Markov Chain Monte Carlo.

Gibbs sampling and random-walk Metropolis–Hastings are powerful, but they scale poorly with dimension. A random-walk proposal explores parameter space by diffusing, which requires O(D2)O(D^2)O(D2) steps to produce an independent sample in a DDD-dimensional space.

Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) uses gradient information to make large, directed moves through parameter space, reducing that cost to roughly O(D5/4)O(D^{5/4})O(D5/4). The key idea is to interpret the negative log-probability as a potential energy surface and simulate the physics of a frictionless particle sliding over it.

In this chapter we:

  • Motivate gradient-based proposals starting from the Metropolis-Adjusted Langevin Algorithm (MALA)

  • Derive Hamilton’s equations and their key properties

  • Implement the leapfrog integrator, the numerical engine of HMC

  • Assemble the full HMC algorithm and verify it on a 2D correlated Gaussian

  • Compare HMC against random-walk MH as dimension grows

Why Random Walks Are Slow¶

In Metropolis–Hastings with a symmetric Gaussian proposal q(θ′∣θ)=N(θ′∣θ,τ2I)q(\mbtheta' \mid \mbtheta) = \cN(\mbtheta' \mid \mbtheta, \tau^2 \mbI)q(θ′∣θ)=N(θ′∣θ,τ2I), the chain diffuses through Θ\ThetaΘ. To maintain a reasonable acceptance rate, the proposal s.d. τ\tauτ must be comparable to the smallest marginal s.d. of the posterior. Each step is then roughly uncorrelated only in that most-constrained direction, while in wider directions many steps are needed to traverse the distribution. The upshot: O(D2)O(D^2)O(D2) gradient evaluations to produce one effectively independent sample Neal, 2011.

Metropolis-Adjusted Langevin Algorithm¶

One improvement is to drift the proposal toward high-probability regions using the gradient:

q(θ′∣θ)=N ⁣(θ+τ∇θlog⁡p(θ,X),  2τ2I).\begin{align} q(\mbtheta' \mid \mbtheta) &= \cN\!\bigl(\mbtheta + \tau \nabla_{\mbtheta} \log p(\mbtheta, \mbX),\; 2\tau^2 \mbI\bigr). \end{align}q(θ′∣θ)​=N(θ+τ∇θ​logp(θ,X),2τ2I).​​
(1)

This Metropolis-Adjusted Langevin Algorithm (MALA) is a discrete-time approximation to the Langevin diffusion SDE. It needs only O(D4/3)O(D^{4/3})O(D4/3) gradient evaluations per independent sample — better than random-walk MH, but we can do better still by taking multiple gradient steps per proposal. That is the idea behind HMC.

Hamiltonian Dynamics¶

Setup¶

Think of the negative log-probability as a potential energy surface. Introduce an auxiliary momentum variable p∈RD\mbp \in \reals^Dp∈RD (one per parameter) and define the Hamiltonian:

H(q,p)=U(q)+K(p),U(q)=−log⁡p(θ=q,X),K(p)=12p⊤M−1p,\begin{align} H(\mbq, \mbp) &= U(\mbq) + K(\mbp), \qquad U(\mbq) = -\log p(\mbtheta = \mbq, \mbX), \qquad K(\mbp) = \tfrac{1}{2}\mbp^\top \mbM^{-1} \mbp, \end{align}H(q,p)​=U(q)+K(p),U(q)=−logp(θ=q,X),K(p)=21​p⊤M−1p,​​
(2)

where M\mbMM is a symmetric positive-definite mass matrix (typically M=mI\mbM = m\mbIM=mI).

The joint distribution over position and momentum factorizes:

p(q,p)∝exp⁡{−H(q,p)}=p(θ=q∣X)⏟posterior×N(p∣0,M)⏟auxiliary.\begin{align} p(\mbq, \mbp) \propto \exp\{-H(\mbq, \mbp)\} = \underbrace{p(\mbtheta = \mbq \mid \mbX)}_{\text{posterior}} \times \underbrace{\cN(\mbp \mid \mbzero, \mbM)}_{\text{auxiliary}}. \end{align}p(q,p)∝exp{−H(q,p)}=posteriorp(θ=q∣X)​​×auxiliaryN(p∣0,M)​​.​​
(3)

Marginalizing out p\mbpp recovers the posterior, so samples of q\mbqq from this joint distribution are posterior samples.

Hamilton’s Equations¶

The state (q,p)(\mbq, \mbp)(q,p) evolves in continuous time according to:

dqddt=−∂H∂pd=[M−1p]d,dpddt=−∂H∂qd=−∂U∂qd.\begin{align} \frac{dq_d}{dt} &= \phantom{-}\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_d} = [\mbM^{-1}\mbp]_d, \\ \frac{dp_d}{dt} &= -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_d} = -\frac{\partial U}{\partial q_d}. \end{align}dtdqd​​dtdpd​​​=−∂pd​∂H​=[M−1p]d​,=−∂qd​∂H​=−∂qd​∂U​.​​
(4)

Three key properties of these dynamics make them useful for MCMC:

  1. Reversibility: negating p\mbpp reverses the trajectory, making the proposal symmetric after a final momentum flip.

  2. Conservation of energy: dHdt=0\frac{dH}{dt} = 0dtdH​=0, so the Hamiltonian is constant along trajectories. If the dynamics could be simulated exactly, every HMC proposal would be accepted.

  3. Volume preservation: the divergence of the vector field is zero (since ∂2H∂qd∂pd=∂2H∂pd∂qd\frac{\partial^2 H}{\partial q_d \partial p_d} = \frac{\partial^2 H}{\partial p_d \partial q_d}∂qd​∂pd​∂2H​=∂pd​∂qd​∂2H​), so Liouville’s theorem applies and there is no Jacobian correction in the acceptance probability.

1D Example: Harmonic Oscillator¶

When D=1D=1D=1, U(q)=12q2U(q) = \tfrac{1}{2}q^2U(q)=21​q2, and m=1m=1m=1, Hamilton’s equations become q˙=p\dot{q} = pq˙​=p, p˙=−q\dot{p} = -qp˙​=−q — a rotation in (q,p)(q, p)(q,p) space. The exact solution is z(t)=eJtz(0)\mbz(t) = e^{\mbJ t} \mbz(0)z(t)=eJtz(0) where J=[01−10]\mbJ = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]J=[0−1​10​], tracing a circle of constant Hamiltonian.

The Leapfrog Integrator¶

Euler vs. Leapfrog¶

Naïve Euler integration updates position and momentum simultaneously:

p(t+ϵ)=p(t)−ϵ∇qU(q(t)),q(t+ϵ)=q(t)+ϵM−1p(t).\begin{align} \mbp(t+\epsilon) &= \mbp(t) - \epsilon \nabla_{\mbq} U(\mbq(t)), \\ \mbq(t+\epsilon) &= \mbq(t) + \epsilon \mbM^{-1} \mbp(t). \end{align}p(t+ϵ)q(t+ϵ)​=p(t)−ϵ∇q​U(q(t)),=q(t)+ϵM−1p(t).​​
(5)

Euler integration does not preserve volume and introduces systematic energy drift even for small ϵ\epsilonϵ.

The leapfrog integrator interleaves half-steps for momentum with full steps for position:

p(t+ϵ2)=p(t)−ϵ2∇qU(q(t)),q(t+ϵ)=q(t)+ϵM−1p(t+ϵ2),p(t+ϵ)=p(t+ϵ2)−ϵ2∇qU(q(t+ϵ)).\begin{align} \mbp(t + \tfrac{\epsilon}{2}) &= \mbp(t) - \tfrac{\epsilon}{2} \nabla_{\mbq} U(\mbq(t)), \\ \mbq(t + \epsilon) &= \mbq(t) + \epsilon \mbM^{-1} \mbp(t + \tfrac{\epsilon}{2}), \\ \mbp(t + \epsilon) &= \mbp(t + \tfrac{\epsilon}{2}) - \tfrac{\epsilon}{2} \nabla_{\mbq} U(\mbq(t + \epsilon)). \end{align}p(t+2ϵ​)q(t+ϵ)p(t+ϵ)​=p(t)−2ϵ​∇q​U(q(t)),=q(t)+ϵM−1p(t+2ϵ​),=p(t+2ϵ​)−2ϵ​∇q​U(q(t+ϵ)).​​
(6)

Each update is a shear transformation (only some variables change, by amounts depending on the others), which has unit Jacobian. Composing shears preserves volume exactly. LLL consecutive leapfrog steps cost L+1L+1L+1 gradient evaluations (L−1L-1L−1 full steps sandwiched between two half-steps).

def leapfrog(q, p, grad_U, ε, L, M_inv):
    """Simulate L leapfrog steps of size ε.

    Args:
        q:       (D,) position
        p:       (D,) momentum
        grad_U:  callable, returns gradient of U at q
        ε:       step size
        L:       number of leapfrog steps
        M_inv:   (D, D) inverse mass matrix

    Returns:
        q, p after L steps
    """
    p = p - (ε / 2) * grad_U(q)
    for _ in range(L - 1):
        q = q + ε * (M_inv @ p)
        p = p - ε * grad_U(q)
    q = q + ε * (M_inv @ p)
    p = p - (ε / 2) * grad_U(q)
    return q, p

The HMC Algorithm¶

Using Hamiltonian Dynamics as a Proposal¶

HMC is Metropolis–Hastings on the joint distribution p(q,p)p(\mbq, \mbp)p(q,p) with proposals generated by leapfrog integration.

Algorithm (one HMC iteration starting from q\mbqq):

  1. Resample momentum: p∼N(0,M)\mbp \sim \cN(\mbzero, \mbM)p∼N(0,M).

  2. Leapfrog LLL steps: integrate Hamilton’s equations for time LϵL\epsilonLϵ to obtain proposed (q∗,p∗)(\mbq^*, \mbp^*)(q∗,p∗).

  3. Flip momentum: p∗←−p∗\mbp^* \leftarrow -\mbp^*p∗←−p∗ (makes the proposal symmetric).

  4. Accept/reject:

a=min⁡ ⁣{1,  exp⁡(H(q,p)−H(q∗,p∗))}.\begin{align} a = \min\!\left\{1,\; \exp\bigl(H(\mbq, \mbp) - H(\mbq^*, \mbp^*)\bigr) \right\}. \end{align}a=min{1,exp(H(q,p)−H(q∗,p∗))}.​​
(7)

If HHH were exactly conserved (exact dynamics), a=1a = 1a=1 always. In practice the leapfrog introduces O(ϵ2)O(\epsilon^2)O(ϵ2) energy error, so some rejections occur, and a smaller ϵ\epsilonϵ gives a higher acceptance rate at the cost of smaller steps.

Tuning¶

The two hyperparameters are the step size ϵ\epsilonϵ and the number of steps LLL:

  • ϵ\epsilonϵ controls accuracy: smaller ϵ\epsilonϵ → higher acceptance rate but smaller moves.

  • LLL controls how far each proposal travels: larger LLL → more decorrelated samples but more gradient evaluations per step.

A target acceptance rate of ~0.65 is commonly used for step-size adaptation. The No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS; Hoffman & Gelman, 2014) automatically adapts LLL by continuing the trajectory until it starts turning back on itself — eliminating the need to tune LLL by hand. NUTS is the default sampler in Stan and Pyro.

def hmc(q0, log_joint, grad_U, ε, L, M, num_samples):
    """Hamiltonian Monte Carlo sampler.

    Args:
        q0:          (D,) initial position
        log_joint:   callable: q -> scalar log p(q, X)
        grad_U:      callable: q -> (D,) gradient of -log_joint
        ε:           leapfrog step size
        L:           number of leapfrog steps per proposal
        M:           (D, D) mass matrix
        num_samples: number of HMC iterations

    Returns:
        (num_samples+1, D) tensor of samples (including q0),
        acceptance rate
    """
    M_inv  = torch.linalg.inv(M)
    M_chol = torch.linalg.cholesky(M)
    q       = q0.clone()
    samples = [q.clone()]
    n_accept = 0

    for _ in range(num_samples):
        p = M_chol @ torch.randn_like(q)
        H_curr = -log_joint(q) + 0.5 * p @ M_inv @ p

        q_prop, p_prop = leapfrog(q.clone(), p.clone(), grad_U, ε, L, M_inv)
        H_prop = -log_joint(q_prop) + 0.5 * p_prop @ M_inv @ p_prop

        if torch.rand(1).item() < torch.exp(H_curr - H_prop).item():
            q = q_prop
            n_accept += 1
        samples.append(q.clone())

    return torch.stack(samples), n_accept / num_samples

Comparison with Random-Walk MH¶

For random-walk MH on the same target, the proposal s.d. must be small enough to not overshoot the narrow direction (σ≈1−ρ2≈0.31\sigma \approx \sqrt{1-\rho^2} \approx 0.31σ≈1−ρ2​≈0.31), which makes exploration of the wide direction slow.

# Random-walk MH on same 2D Gaussian
def rwmh(q0, log_joint, σ_prop, num_samples):
    D = q0.shape[0]
    q = q0.clone()
    samples  = [q.clone()]
    n_accept = 0
    for _ in range(num_samples):
        q_prop = q + σ_prop * torch.randn(D)
        log_α  = log_joint(q_prop) - log_joint(q)
        if torch.rand(1).item() < torch.exp(log_α).item():
            q = q_prop
            n_accept += 1
        samples.append(q.clone())
    return torch.stack(samples), n_accept / num_samples

Scaling with Dimension¶

Benefits of Avoiding Random Walks¶

For a DDD-dimensional isotropic Gaussian target the cost of obtaining one independent sample scales as:

AlgorithmGradient evaluations
Random-walk MHO(D2)O(D^2)O(D2)
MALAO(D4/3)O(D^{4/3})O(D4/3)
HMCO(D5/4)O(D^{5/4})O(D5/4)

The advantage of HMC grows with the condition number of the posterior covariance. Random-walk MH must step at the scale of the most constrained dimension, requiring κ\kappaκ steps in the widest direction (where κ\kappaκ is the condition number). HMC integrates the gradient to traverse the wide direction efficiently in κ\sqrt{\kappa}κ​ leapfrog steps.

Practical Considerations¶

Step-Size Adaptation¶

The step size ϵ\epsilonϵ must be tuned:

  • Too large → large energy errors → low acceptance rate.

  • Too small → small moves → slow mixing.

A target acceptance rate of ~0.65 is recommended by Neal, 2011. A simple strategy during warm-up: increase ϵ\epsilonϵ when the acceptance rate exceeds the target, decrease when below. The dual averaging scheme of Hoffman & Gelman, 2014 provides a principled adaptive version used by default in Pyro and Stan.

No-U-Turn Sampler¶

Choosing LLL by hand is also inconvenient. The No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) Hoffman & Gelman, 2014 extends the trajectory dynamically until it begins turning back (the inner product p⋅(q−q0)<0\mbp \cdot (\mbq - {\mbq}_0) < 0p⋅(q−q0​)<0), then selects a sample from along the trajectory using slice sampling. NUTS:

  • Eliminates the need to tune LLL.

  • Adapts ϵ\epsilonϵ automatically during warm-up.

  • Is the default backend for Stan, PyMC, and Pyro’s NUTS kernel.

An interactive visualization of HMC and NUTS: https://chi-feng.github.io/mcmc-demo/app.html

Conclusion¶

HMC exploits gradient information to make large, coherent moves through parameter space, vastly outperforming random-walk methods in high dimensions. Key points:

  • The potential energy is U(q)=−log⁡p(q,X)U(\mbq) = -\log p(\mbq, \mbX)U(q)=−logp(q,X); auxiliary momentum variables p∼N(0,M)\mbp \sim \cN(\mbzero, \mbM)p∼N(0,M) complete the Hamiltonian system.

  • Hamiltonian dynamics are reversible, energy-conserving, and volume-preserving — exactly the properties needed for a valid MCMC kernel.

  • The leapfrog integrator preserves these properties approximately, introducing only O(ϵ2)O(\epsilon^2)O(ϵ2) energy error per step.

  • The Metropolis correction accounts for numerical integration error, ensuring exact invariance with respect to the target.

  • NUTS removes the need to hand-tune LLL and is the practical choice for most applications.
